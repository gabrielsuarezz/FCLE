üìú Founding Documents

Magna Carta (1215)

Origin: English document signed by King John

Key Principles:

  • Limited the power of the king
  • Established that everyone, including the king, is subject to the law
  • Protected certain legal rights (due process)
  • No taxation without representation

Influence on America: Inspired concepts of limited government and rule of law in the U.S. Constitution

Declaration of Independence (1776)

Purpose: Announced separation from Great Britain

Key Idea: "All men are created equal" with unalienable rights to life, liberty, and pursuit of happiness

Author: Thomas Jefferson

Philosophical Influence: John Locke (NOT James Madison) - natural rights theory

Why colonists rebelled: British tax policies violated their political rights (taxation without representation)

Articles of Confederation (1781)

Purpose: First constitution of the United States

Weakness: Created weak central government, no power to tax or regulate trade

Result: Replaced by the Constitution

Northwest Ordinances (1787)

Purpose: Established how western territories could become states

Key Provisions:

  • Set requirements for statehood
  • Guaranteed civil liberties in new territories
  • Prohibited slavery in the Northwest Territory (north of Ohio River)
  • Promoted public education

Historical Impact: The prohibition of slavery clause inspired the 13th Amendment's language

The Constitution (1787)

Written: Constitutional Convention in Philadelphia, 1787

Author: James Madison (Father of the Constitution)

Structure: Established three branches of government

The Great Compromise (Connecticut Compromise): Resolved dispute between large and small states - created bicameral legislature with Senate (equal representation - 2 per state) and House (proportional representation based on population)

Flexibility: Can be amended (27 amendments so far)

The Bill of Rights (1791)

Purpose: First 10 amendments protecting individual liberties

Author: James Madison wrote these amendments

Key Rights: Speech, religion, press, assembly, bear arms, fair trial

Impact: Fundamental protections against government overreach

üëë Contrasting Government Systems

Divine Right of Kings

Definition: The belief that monarchs derive their authority directly from God, not from the people

Characteristics:

  • Power achieved through inheritance
  • Same ruling family for generations/centuries
  • Hierarchical social status (nobles, commoners, serfs)
  • Finite freedoms and rights for citizens
  • Single established state religion
  • King/Queen not subject to earthly authority

Contrast with American Democracy: America rejected this concept - the Declaration of Independence states power comes from "consent of the governed," not divine appointment

üéØ Remember: The Constitution begins with "We the People" - emphasizing that government power comes from citizens, not from kings or divine right.

Ensure domestic tranquility

Keep peace within the country

Provide for common defense

Protect the nation

Promote general welfare

Help people live well

Secure liberty

Protect freedom for future generations

üèõÔ∏è Ancient Influences on American Democracy

Ancient Greece (Athens) - 6th Century B.C.E.

Contribution: Direct Democracy

Key Features:

  • Citizens voted directly on laws (similar to modern referendums)
  • Two assemblies divided government powers - showing early separation of powers
  • Political rights guaranteed for male citizens
  • Limited government authority - demonstrated how societies develop by restraining government power
  • Electing leaders (governors, officials) rather than hereditary rule

üéØ Most Similar Modern U.S. Practice: The REFERENDUM (not the presidential election) - where citizens vote directly on laws and policy questions, just like Athenian citizens voted directly on issues. Examples include ballot initiatives and state referendums.

‚úì Ancient Greeks Would Support: Electing governors - they pioneered the concept of citizens choosing their leaders through elections rather than accepting hereditary rulers or appointed officials.

Ancient Rome

Contribution: Republican Form of Government

Key Features:

  • Representative government (not direct democracy)
  • Senate and assemblies
  • Written laws and legal codes
  • Checks on executive power
  • Republic based on popular sovereignty

Modern Example: U.S. Republic with elected representatives in Congress

Montesquieu

üìã The Seven Articles of the Constitution

Article I - The Legislative Branch

Establishes: Congress (Senate and House of Representatives)

Powers Include:

  • Coin money and regulate its value
  • Make treaties (with Senate approval)
  • Levy import duties and taxes
  • Regulate interstate and foreign commerce
  • Declare war
  • Establish post offices
  • Create federal courts below the Supreme Court
  • Make all laws "necessary and proper" (Elastic Clause)

Senate: 100 members (2 per state), 6-year terms

House: 435 members (by population), 2-year terms

Article II - The Executive Branch

Establishes: The Presidency and Executive Branch

Powers Include:

  • Commander in Chief of the military
  • Make treaties (with 2/3 Senate approval)
  • Appoint ambassadors, judges, and cabinet members
  • Grant pardons and reprieves
  • Sign or veto legislation
  • Ensure laws are faithfully executed

Qualifications: 35+ years old, natural-born citizen, 14 years residency

Term: 4 years (maximum 2 terms per 22nd Amendment)

Article III - The Judicial Branch

Establishes: Supreme Court and federal court system

Powers Include:

  • Interpret laws and the Constitution
  • Try cases involving federal law, treaties, and the Constitution
  • Resolve disputes between states
  • Handle cases involving ambassadors and public ministers

Appointments: Lifetime tenure for federal judges

Article IV - States' Relations

Covers: Relationships between states and federal government

  • Full Faith and Credit Clause - states must respect other states' laws and records
  • Extradition between states
  • Admission of new states
  • Federal protection of states

Article V - Amendment Process

Describes: How to amend the Constitution

  • Proposal: 2/3 vote in both houses of Congress OR national convention
  • Ratification: 3/4 of state legislatures OR state conventions

Article VI - Federal Power

Contains: The Supremacy Clause

Supremacy Clause: The Constitution, federal laws, and treaties are the "supreme law of the land" - they override state laws when there is a conflict

Also includes: No religious test for federal office

Article VII - Ratification

Required: 9 of 13 states needed to ratify the Constitution

Achieved: June 21, 1788 (when New Hampshire became the 9th state)

üéØ Exam Tip: Remember Article I = Legislative, Article II = Executive, Article III = Judicial. Article I grants Congress the power to coin money, make treaties, and levy import duties.

‚úä Rights and Responsibilities

The Bill of Rights (First 10 Amendments) - 1791

üí° Important: Written by James Madison and ratified in 1791, the Bill of Rights protects individual liberties from government interference.
1st Amendment

Five Freedoms:

  • Religion (free exercise and no establishment)
  • Speech
  • Press
  • Assembly (peaceful gatherings, demonstrations)
  • Petition the government

Note: Assembly means the right to demonstrate, NOT riot. Peaceful protest is protected.

2nd Amendment

Right to keep and bear arms

Context: Written to ensure states could maintain militias

3rd Amendment

No quartering soldiers in homes without owner's consent

Inspired by: Declaration of Independence complaint about British standing armies

Limits: Quartering of soldiers only allowed in specific wartime circumstances

4th Amendment

Protection from unreasonable searches and seizures

Requires: Search warrant based on probable cause

Example: Police need a warrant to search hotel room contents

Does NOT require warrant for: Plain view, searches during arrest, border crossings

5th Amendment

Multiple protections:

  • Due process of law
  • No self-incrimination ("pleading the Fifth")
  • No double jeopardy (tried twice for same crime)
  • Grand jury for serious crimes
  • Just compensation for property taken by government
6th Amendment

Criminal trial rights:

  • Speedy and public trial
  • Impartial jury
  • Right to an attorney (see Gideon v. Wainwright)
  • Right to confront witnesses
  • Compel witnesses to testify
7th Amendment

Right to jury trial in civil cases (not just criminal)

Civil law cases: Defamation, libel, slander, property damage, breach of contract

8th Amendment

Prohibits:

  • Excessive bail
  • Excessive fines
  • Cruel and unusual punishment
9th Amendment

Rights not specifically listed in the Constitution are still retained by the people

Meaning: The Bill of Rights doesn't list ALL rights - people have additional rights beyond those enumerated

10th Amendment

Powers not delegated to federal government belong to states or the people

Examples of state powers: Education, police, driver's licenses, marriage laws

Federal powers: Coin money, declare war, treaties, interstate commerce

Important Later Amendments

13th Amendment (1865)

Abolished slavery

14th Amendment (1868)

Equal protection and due process for all citizens

15th Amendment (1870)

Voting rights regardless of race

19th Amendment (1920)

Women's right to vote

26th Amendment (1971)

Voting age lowered to 18

Rights of Citizens

Freedom of Expression

Speak, write, worship as you choose

Fair Trial

Due process and legal representation

Vote

Choose representatives in elections

Run for Office

Seek elected positions

Responsibilities of Citizens

Vote

Participate in elections

Serve on Jury

When called for jury duty

Obey Laws

Follow federal, state, and local laws

Pay Taxes

Support government services

Defend Nation

Serve in military if called

Stay Informed

Know about issues and candidates

üí° Key Concept: Rights and responsibilities go hand in hand. Citizens have freedoms protected by law, but also duties to maintain a functioning democracy.

‚öñÔ∏è Landmark Supreme Court Cases

Marbury v. Madison (1803)

Established: Judicial review - the power of courts to determine if laws are constitutional

Plessy v. Ferguson (1896)

Ruling: "Separate but equal" - allowed segregation of public facilities (later overturned)

Brown v. Board of Education (1954)

Overturned: Plessy v. Ferguson - declared school segregation unconstitutional

Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)

Right to Counsel: Defendants have the right to an attorney, even if they cannot afford one

Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

Miranda Rights: Police must inform arrested persons of their rights

Korematsu v. United States (1944)

Controversial: Upheld Japanese internment during WWII - civil rights vs. national security

üìã Key Historical Documents

Mayflower Compact (1620)

Significance: First governing document of Plymouth Colony. Established idea of self-government and rule by consent.

Impact: Set precedent for U.S. Constitution's concept of government by the people

Northwest Ordinances (1787)

Purpose: Established how territories could become states

Emancipation Proclamation (1863)

Issued by: President Abraham Lincoln during Civil War

Effect: Declared slaves in Confederate states to be free

üìú Important Legislation & Doctrines

Interstate Commerce Act (1887)

Purpose: First federal law regulating private industry

Coverage: Regulated railroads and interstate transportation

Created: Interstate Commerce Commission (ICC)

Significance: Established federal power to regulate commerce between states

Monroe Doctrine (1823)

President: James Monroe

Main Principle: Prohibited future European colonization in the Western Hemisphere

Message: "America for Americans" - European powers should not interfere in the Americas

U.S. Position: Would view European colonization attempts as acts of aggression

‚ö†Ô∏è Exam Note: The doctrine PROHIBITED European colonization, it did NOT justify U.S. annexation of overseas territories (common wrong answer)

New Deal (1933-1939)

President: Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR)

Purpose: Combat the Great Depression

Key Programs:

  • Work programs (WPA, CCC)
  • Social Security
  • Banking reforms
  • Economic security measures

Philosophy: "What do people want? Work and security"

‚öñÔ∏è Watergate Break-In & Scandal (1972-1974)

The Watergate Scandal

What Happened:

In 1972, burglars connected to President Nixon's re-election campaign broke into the Democratic National Committee headquarters at the Watergate Hotel complex in Washington, D.C.

The Cover-Up:

  • Nixon administration attempted to cover up its involvement
  • Investigations revealed abuse of power and obstruction of justice
  • Nixon claimed executive privilege and refused to comply with subpoenas

Nixon's Famous Quote:

"Well, when the president does it, that means that it is not illegal"

This statement contradicts: The Rule of Law - the principle that everyone, including the President, must follow the law and no one is above the law

üéØ MOST IMPORTANT CONSEQUENCE: Resignation of the President of the United States (August 9, 1974)

Historical Significance:

  • First and only U.S. President to resign from office
  • Demonstrated that rule of law applies to everyone, even the President
  • Showed the strength of checks and balances
  • Proved that the Constitution works to limit executive power
  • Reinforced importance of free press and investigations

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Wrong Answer: "Creation of levees around coastal cities" - this has nothing to do with Watergate!
